Why Global SLOs Can Be Misleading

Global SLOs look at the entire system’s performance in aggregate. While this gives a high-level overview, it can hide localized failures that still impact users.

Example:

  • Your system handles 1 million requests daily
  • 990,000 of those requests are flawless
  • 10,000 requests to a specific service (e.g., /checkout) fail
  • The global error rate = 1%, which may not trigger an alert depending on your threshold

But for users trying to complete a purchase, the experience is completely broken.

Benefits of Per-Bucket SLO Tracking

  • 🧠 Better diagnostics: You can immediately see which feature or service is affected
  • 🔁 Faster mitigation: The responsible team can react without needing to sift through logs
  • 📊 Custom thresholds: High-risk paths (like payments) can have stricter targets
  • 🎯 Supports prioritization: Focus error budget and fixes on what matters most to users

Tips for Implementing Buckets

  • ✅ Start with critical user flows or high-traffic endpoints
  • ✅ Make sure each bucket has enough traffic to be statistically significant
  • ✅ Use tags or labels in your monitoring system to group traffic automatically
  • ✅ Align buckets with team ownership to simplify accountability

Common Pitfalls

  • ❌ Too many buckets: Leads to alert fatigue and fragmented visibility
  • ❌ Overlapping buckets: Make attribution difficult and confusing
  • ❌ Buckets with low volume: Can generate noise and false positives
  • ❌ Unclear ownership: Alerts fall through the cracks if no team is assigned

Practical Advice

Use tooling like Prometheus labels, Datadog tags, or Azure Monitor dimensions to categorize traffic automatically.

Set alerts with:

  • A threshold (e.g., 0.1% error rate)
  • A minimum request volume (e.g., at least 1,000 requests in the window)

This helps prevent alerts from firing on low-traffic paths where even one or two errors can spike the rate.
